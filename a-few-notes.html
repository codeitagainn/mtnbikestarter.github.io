A Few Notes

Technology advancements

10 years ago getting a mountain bike that could handle real terrain for under $1000 wasn’t a real option. For that reason the sport was out of reach for a lot of people interested.

Luckily that is not the case anymore. Mountain biking technology continues to evolve at faster rates than ever and that benefits everyone from 10 year biking veterans to you.

In the past the most expensive bikes were the only ones with the technology capable of handling real MTB terrain without breaking down or leaving your body feeling like you had been jackhammering all day.

Today all trail bikes that cost thousands of dollars can handle turns with amazing precision at breakneck speeds and on drops you have to double check that you even landed the suspension is so advanced. But this also means that the more affordable bikes now have the ability to take on real terrain and real drops, so the option to get started without breaking the bank is real.

Hardtail v full suspension

The hardtail vs full suspension debate is possibly the most wildly debated MTB topic there is. Try Googling “Hardtail vs Full Suspension” and you will not find a shortage of opinions.

A TLDR of the debate might look like this:

Hardtail pros - Less maintenance, high quality components at the same price point of the full suspension bikes.

Full suspension pros - smoother ride due to rear suspension, more grip on turns.

Generally if you are interested in buying a bike for under $1000 you will be better off going with a hardtail. In addition to getting a higher quality bike, you will also become a more technical rider due to not having the rear suspension. It is certainly an easier transition from hardtail to full suspension rider than the other way around.

If you do want the added comfort of a full suspension bike right away we recommend entering the sport with at least $1500 to spend on a bike.

Buy a bike you can grow with

An often voiced opinion from riders is to buy a bike for the biker you want to be a year from now.

We think that sounds nice, but its extremely difficult to predict what kind of rider you will be a year from now.

Right now you might be thinking you want to fly down an enduro course at breakneck speeds a year from now, but then 12 months later you decide that cross country racing suits you better.

We tend to think this argument underestimates the complexity of human beings and the continuously changing an evolving nature of ourselves.

Taking in the human being factor, we take the stance that you should buy the bike that you think suits you now and you’ll have a far better understanding of the rider you want to be after you hit the trails a few times.

Female Specific Bikes

You don’t necessarily need one. Women’s bikes were made to factor in differences in geometry. Generally, women’s legs are proportionally longer then men of the same height. This lead to the development of women’s bikes.

Given the flexible nature of the bike saddles and handlebars many of these differences are negligible.

Be cautious when purchasing a woman’s bike. Part quality can be worse for the same specifications and price. Double check that the women’s bike you are looking at has the same kit as the mens for the same price.

These notes are not universal, so use your own judgement when choosing a bike. If you have a good local bike shop, go there and try out a few before selecting the right bike for you.
